# English Voice

## Core Voice

- Write in clear, native English with a visible point of view.
- Lead with a claim, tension, or problem, not generic background.
- Prefer concrete examples, explicit tradeoffs, and memorable framing over broad summaries.

## What To Borrow From The Five English Samples

- `Choose Boring Technology`: use one memorable framing device when it clarifies the decision.
- `Parse, don't validate`: build the concept from a worked example, then sharpen the definition.
- `Things You Should Never Do, Part I`: state the contrarian claim early and defend it with causality.
- `Write code that is easy to delete`: let each section push the thesis forward, not just add trivia.
- `The Grug Brained Developer`: use repetition and simplification only as clarity tools, never as a gimmick to copy.

## How To Use This Voice In `essay` Mode

- Open with the sharpest statement the article can honestly support.
- Let the article revolve around one thesis, not several adjacent ideas.
- Make each section do one of three things:
  - explain the model
  - compare alternatives
  - derive a practical consequence
- Use first person only when it adds earned judgment or project context.
- End with a heuristic, decision rule, or practical implication the reader can carry forward.

## How To Use This Voice In `guide` Mode

- Follow the portfolio's existing guide style: problem first, scope second, explicit sectioning throughout.
- Use question-led or decision-led H2 headings.
- Name the real files, commands, modules, and constraints when that improves trust.
- Prefer "here is how this system is wired" over vague best-practice prose.
- Keep the tone direct and specific; guides should feel inspectable.
- If the topic is fast-moving, time-sensitive, or shaped by current platform behavior, open with that live context instead of article housekeeping.
- If the post belongs to a series, keep the series framing secondary unless the article's primary job is navigation.
- For large topics, add an explicit order of operations so the reader knows what to do first versus what to tune later.

## Sentence And Rhythm Guidance

- Favor medium-length sentences with deliberate emphasis, not meandering paragraphs.
- Use short paragraphs when changing gears or sharpening a contrast.
- Give lists a clear reason to exist: criteria, tradeoffs, steps, or guarantees.
- Use bold text sparingly to mark a key decision or phrase.
- Make the main claim quotable in one sentence.

## Avoid

- Generic "in today's world" openings.
- Literal translation from Chinese cadence.
- Overexplaining obvious context before making the point.
- Borrowing source essays' iconic wording, slogans, or personas.
- Mimicking `grugbrain`'s caveman voice. Borrow memorability, not the costume.
- Sounding like marketing copy or LinkedIn thought leadership.
- Forcing unnecessary variation across `title`, `seoTitle`, `description`, and `seoDescription` when one precise search-facing phrasing is stronger.

## Quality Check

- Can the reader summarize the thesis after the first two paragraphs?
- Does each section either explain, compare, or decide?
- Are the examples specific enough to be believable?
- Would an engineer know what to do or what to think differently after reading it?
